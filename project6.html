Defining sensation for the painless

Resense

At Stanford Immerse the Bay 2025 my team and I built an Augmented Reality system designed to help people with congenital insensitivity to pain perceive environmental dangers through real-time haptic feedback. This innovative solution addresses a critical need for individuals who cannot feel pain, providing them with essential sensory information to navigate the world safely and confidently.

Problem Space

Congenital Insensitivity to Pain (CIP) is a rare genetic condition where pain signals never reach the brain, even though touch senses are normal. Without pain feedback, CIP patients lack the internal danger threshold that prevents burns, cuts, broken bones, joint pain and long-duration tissue damage.

Solution

We built a training and guidance tool that combines the Meta Quest headset, Arduino, ESP 32, servo motor, 5 flex sensors for each finger,and a thermoster. We combined all of this hardware to make a haptic glove to give CIP patients a new feedback channel that signals the risk before damage occurs and guides through daily tasks. This glove creates customizable haptic feedback patterns, (ticks, pulses, different rhtyms) letting the system translate heat, pressure, or unsafe duration into a new sensory language the user can understand.

Visually, the haptic gloves grip strength data and temperature data is displayed on the Quest. For temperature the Quest directly edits the passthrough changing to red or blue. For grip strength the values are displayed on the Quest UI.
